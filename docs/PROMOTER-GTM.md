# Promoter Go-To-Market: Data Sovereignty as the Wedge

Status: strategy of record. Owner: founder. Last updated: 2026-05-30.

EventLinqs wins organisers by giving them something Eventbrite and Ticketmaster
structurally will not: ownership of their own audience, their own money, and
honest pricing. This document is the go-to-market for the organiser side of the
marketplace. The attendee-reporting and export feature is the first proof point.

## Who we target: the discontented middle

We do not start at the top (arena tours, locked to Ticketmaster) or the very
bottom (one-off free community events, well served by free tools). We target the
discontented middle: mid-tier promoters and independents who sell real volume,
care about their fans, and are actively unhappy with their current platform.

- Mid-tier promoters running rooms and events of 500 to 5,000 capacity.
- Independent festivals: single-stage and boutique multi-day events that are too
  big for a spreadsheet and too independent for an enterprise contract.
- Regional club nights and touring promoters who run a recurring calendar across
  several cities and want to keep their crowd between events.

Why this segment:

- They have a real, repeat audience, so audience ownership compounds in value.
- They feel the fee pain directly because margins are thin.
- They are reachable: they are active in their scene and talk to each other, so
  word of mouth moves fast within a community.
- They are underserved: Eventbrite treats them like a form, Ticketmaster will not
  return their calls, and Humanitix does not lead on audience ownership.

## The three pillars

The whole pitch reduces to three promises, in plain language.

### 1. You own your fans

Every buyer name and email belongs to the organiser, not the platform. The
attendee list is viewable in the dashboard and exportable any time as CSV, Excel,
or a printable door list. EventLinqs will never market another organiser's event
to your buyers, never sell or rent your list, and never share it across
organisers. This is enforced in code (row-level security isolates every
organiser's data) and written into the organiser terms. Eventbrite and
Ticketmaster sit between the promoter and the fan on purpose. We do the opposite.

### 2. We do not hold your money

Funds settle to the organiser through their own connected payment account on a
clear payout schedule. We are not a float that sits on a promoter's takings for
weeks. The promoter's money is the promoter's money.

### 3. No hidden fees

All-in pricing shown from the first click. The fee is one honest line: a small
percentage plus a fixed amount per ticket, with the payment processing cost
stated, not buried. No surprise service fees at the end of checkout, and no
retroactive fee changes on past orders.

## Why this beats the incumbents on reporting

The reporting surface is where the "you own your fans" promise becomes tangible.

- A fast, searchable, filterable attendee list in the dashboard, so a promoter
  can answer "who is coming" without downloading anything.
- One-click exports in the formats promoters actually use: CSV and Excel for
  importing into a CRM or email tool, and a clean, printable PDF door list for
  event-day hosts who are not technical.
- A separate orders report with the financial and buyer transaction detail for
  accounting, exportable as CSV and Excel.

The message: your data is one click from leaving with you, which is exactly why
you can trust us to hold it.

## Deferred items (not in the first release)

These are real and on the roadmap, but explicitly out of scope for the first
release so we ship the core promise first.

- Direct Mailchimp sync: push the attendee list straight into a promoter's email
  audience, instead of export then import. The export formats are designed so
  this is an additive enhancement, not a rework.
- Advance payouts: pay a portion of confirmed ticket revenue to the promoter
  before the event, for cash-flow-tight independents.
- Fee-markup tool: let a promoter add their own per-ticket fee on top of the
  EventLinqs fee, with full transparency to the buyer, so the promoter can run
  their own rebate or facility-fee model.

## Sequencing

1. Ship the attendee list, exports, orders report, and the written data-ownership
   commitment (this release).
2. Use it as the lead story in outbound to the discontented middle: "see your
   fans, own your fans, take them with you".
3. Layer the deferred items as retention and upsell once promoters have moved
   their calendar across.

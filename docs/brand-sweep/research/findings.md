## Q1. What headline length pattern do top competitors use?

| Platform     | Surface         | Headline                                                          | Words |
| ------------ | --------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ----- |
| Ticketmaster | Consumer hero   | Tickets for Concerts, Sport, Arts, Theatre, Family, Events, more. | 9     |
| Ticketmaster | Organiser hero  | Define the future of live with us                                 | 7     |
| DICE         | Hero            | We'll get you out more.                                           | 5     |
| Eventbrite   | Consumer hero   | Discover the Best Local Events & Things to Do                     | 8     |
| Eventbrite   | Organiser hero  | Where Event Organizers Grow                                       | 4     |
| Humanitix    | Brand line      | Tickets for good, not greed                                       | 5     |
| Songkick     | Consumer hero   | Your city, your music                                             | 4     |
| RA           | Page positioning| Discover Electronic Music and Events                              | 4     |

**Pattern.** The strongest competitor headlines run 4 to 5 words. The weakest run 8 to 9 and read like meta-descriptions. The 4 to 5 word zone is where positioning lives. Anything over 7 words is a category list, not a hero.

**Rule for EventLinqs.** Hero headlines stay between 4 and 8 words. Mission and sub-taglines may run longer. The locked hero "Every culture. Every event. One platform." is 5 words, sits in the strongest band, and uses parallel triplet rhythm that none of the competitors use.

## Q2. What CTA verbs dominate?

| Verb          | Used by                          | Notes                                                                                     |
| ------------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Find          | Ticketmaster, Eventbrite         | "Find tickets", "Find Events". Functional, undifferentiated.                              |
| Get           | Humanitix                        | "Get Tickets". Direct, action-oriented.                                                   |
| Discover      | Eventbrite (hero), RA            | Used as a category verb, not a CTA. Soft.                                                 |
| Sign up       | Songkick                         | Only competitor leading with account creation. Friction-heavy for a discovery surface.    |
| Work With Us  | Ticketmaster Business            | Relationship-led B2B framing. Distinctive.                                                |
| Submit event  | RA                               | Promoter-side micro-CTA. Functional.                                                      |
| Get started   | Eventbrite organiser             | "Get started for free". Adds the price reassurance to the verb.                           |

**Pattern.** Consumer CTAs cluster on "Find" and "Get". Organiser CTAs cluster on "Get started" with a pricing modifier. Nobody uses verbs of belonging, identity, or invitation.

**Rule for EventLinqs.** Use direct, action-led verbs. Ours: Find, Browse, See, Get, Buy, Save, Send, Share. Avoid Discover (overused, soft), Explore (vague), and Sign up as a primary discovery CTA. Organiser CTAs lead with "Sell tickets" or "Start selling" with the all-in pricing line as a sub-promise.

## Q3. How do competitors handle cultural specificity (or fail to)?

**Ticketmaster** uses categorical verticals: Music, Sport, Arts, Theatre, Family. Zero cultural specificity. The closest the organiser-side gets is the inclusivity line "Ticketmaster and its subsidiaries have solutions for every event that needs a ticket, regardless of size and/or genre." That sentence is doing the work of asserting breadth without naming a single community.

**DICE** sells "your kind of shows". The positioning is taste-coded ("kind of") without naming the taste. Strong on indie and electronic by reputation, silent on it in headline copy.

**Eventbrite** uses verticals (Music, Nightlife, Performing & Visual Arts, Holidays, Dating, Hobbies, Business, Food & Drink). One example footer category, "By Seasonal Moments", lists Halloween, St. Patrick's Day, Christmas, Valentine's. Christian-Western default. No Lunar New Year, no Eid, no Diwali, no Vesak. The omission is the message.

**Humanitix** sells the "tickets for good, not greed" ethical premise. Cultural variety is implied through diversity of listed events (synagogue concert, festival, gala, food and drink) but there is no naming of communities or cultures. The brand promise is impact, not identity.

**RA** is hyper-specific to electronic music and house/techno scene culture. It is the only competitor with genuine cultural authority, but its scope is intentionally narrow.

**Songkick** sells discovery for "your music taste" via genres (Indie & Alt, Electronic, Hip-Hop, Jazz, Metal, Pop, R&B, Rock). All Western/Anglo genres. No Afrobeats, no Amapiano, no Bollywood, no Latin, no K-Pop, no Reggae. Major omission given the size of those audiences globally.

**The gap.** Every mainstream competitor either (a) asserts breadth without naming any culture, (b) names only Christian-Western seasonal moments, or (c) lists only Anglo-American music genres. The Afrobeats fan, the Bollywood fan, the K-Pop fan, the Lunar New Year organiser, the Caribbean carnival promoter has no specific home in the headline copy of any competitor surveyed. Resident Advisor is the only competitor with genuine specificity, and it has chosen one scene.

**Rule for EventLinqs.** Specificity is the differentiator. The cultures list (Afrobeats, Caribbean, Bollywood, Latin, Italian, Filipino, Lunar, Gospel, Amapiano, Comedy, Spanish, K-Pop, Reggae, West African, European, Asian, African, South Asian) appears verbatim in the rendered order on the homepage hero rail and on the Browse and About surfaces. We name. We do not gesture.

## Q4. What footer information architecture do competitors use?

| Platform     | Approx. column count | Column heads                                                                                                                                                              | Notable                                                                                  |
| ------------ | -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Ticketmaster | 7                    | Let's connect; Download Our Apps; Get Help; Our Network; Helpful Links; Be Part Of It; Corporate                                                                          | Heavy on corporate trust signals. "Be Part Of It" is the only non-functional column.     |
| Eventbrite   | 10                   | Event Creation; Event Promotion; Event Hosting; All Features; By Industry; By Use Case; By Seasonal Moments; Learn; Connect; Help & Support                              | SEO-driven. Each column is a category landing-page index.                                |
| Humanitix    | 4                    | Find events; Host events; Use Humanitix; About                                                                                                                            | Tightest information architecture in the set. Easiest to scan at 320px.                  |
| RA           | 2 plus social        | Discover; Get RA apps                                                                                                                                                     | Editorial brand. Footer is intentionally sparse.                                         |
| Songkick     | (not surveyed)       |                                                                                                                                                                            |                                                                                          |
| DICE         | (403 blocked)        |                                                                                                                                                                            |                                                                                          |

**Pattern.** Two strategies coexist: "SEO maximalist" (Eventbrite, Ticketmaster) and "editorial minimalist" (Humanitix, RA). Both work in their context. Mainstream platforms with broad SEO footprints lean maximalist. Brand-led platforms lean minimalist.

**Rule for EventLinqs.** Adopt Humanitix's four-column shape as the baseline because it is mobile-friendly and reads as a publisher rather than a directory. Columns: Find events (cities and cultures), For organisers (sell tickets, pricing, FAQ), About (mission, contact, careers, blog), Help (support, terms, privacy). Cultural specificity goes inside Find events as a sub-list, not as an extra column. SEO landing pages (e.g. afrobeats-events-melbourne) are reachable from there but do not pollute the footer with one-line-per-keyword maximalism.

**Pattern.** No competitor surfaces cultural community as a card-level tag. Cultural framing is either absent (Ticketmaster, Eventbrite, Humanitix, Songkick) or genre-locked to a single scene (RA).

**Rule for EventLinqs.** Event cards carry a cultural tag in the same row as the date and venue. Cultural tags use the locked list (Afrobeats, Caribbean, Bollywood, Latin, Italian, Filipino, Lunar, Gospel, Amapiano, Comedy, Spanish, K-Pop, Reggae, West African, European, Asian, African, South Asian). Tags are not optional metadata; they are the navigation. Missing a cultural tag at create time is a dashboard nudge, not a hard block, but the platform encourages it on every event creation flow.

## Strategic gaps EventLinqs will exploit

1. **Cultural specificity gap.** No competitor names communities. EventLinqs is the only platform that names the cultures it serves on the homepage hero. This is the wedge.
2. **All-in pricing transparency gap.** Eventbrite says "transparent fees" without showing them at first paint. Humanitix talks about booking-fee profits. Ticketmaster has been litigated repeatedly for surprise fees. EventLinqs shows the all-in number on every card and at checkout the price never moves.
3. **Mobile-first information architecture gap.** Eventbrite (10-column footer) and Ticketmaster (7-column footer) collapse poorly on a 390px viewport. Humanitix gets it right. EventLinqs ships a Humanitix-shape footer with sentence-case, plain-English column heads.
4. **WhatsApp-native sharing gap.** Nobody surveyed mentions WhatsApp share. EventLinqs ships WhatsApp share by default in Africa, South Asia, Latin America, and Filipino markets where WhatsApp is the dominant social fabric.
5. **Headline rhythm gap.** Eventbrite and Ticketmaster pad headlines into mini-paragraphs. EventLinqs uses parallel triplet structure ("Every culture. Every event. One platform.") that reads like a chant and works at 320px.
6. **Voice gap.** Songkick is closest to confident-warm but reads American-frat ("good vibes", "down the front"). Humanitix is sincere but sermonising. EventLinqs is Australian-English plain, professional, and warm without being twee.

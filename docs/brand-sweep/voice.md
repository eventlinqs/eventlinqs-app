# EventLinqs Voice

Date:    2026-05-02
Status:  Adopted. Supersedes the voice section of DESIGN-SYSTEM.md.
Scope:   Every public surface. Every email template. Every error message. Every empty state. Every meta description.

This document is the source of truth for how EventLinqs sounds. It governs the words we choose, the rhythm of our sentences, the cadence of our buttons, and the emotion behind our design. If a piece of public copy contradicts this document, the copy is wrong.

---

## 1. Who EventLinqs is

EventLinqs is the ticketing platform built for every culture. Every community, every festival, every event that brings people together. We serve the cultures, audiences, and organisers that mainstream platforms treat as an afterthought, and we do it across Australia, the UK, the US, and Europe at launch.

We are not a marketplace that happens to have international events. We are a platform built around culture from the ground up. The cultures we name on the homepage (Afrobeats, Caribbean, Bollywood, Latin, Italian, Filipino, Lunar, Gospel, Amapiano, Comedy, Spanish, K-Pop, Reggae, West African, European, Asian, African, South Asian) are not categories we added later. They are the spine of the product.

We are not the cheapest. We are not the loudest. We are the platform where the all-in price you see is the price you pay, where Friday night at the carnival has the same craft as Saturday night at the symphony, and where the organiser running their first event gets the same tools as the organiser selling their fiftieth.

## 3. Voice pillars

### Confident, never arrogant
We know what we are. We know what we are not. We say so directly. We do not promise to "revolutionise" anything. Live ticketing is a mature industry. Our advantage is that we built it for cultures the market has ignored, and we built it without the surprise-fee playbook. That is enough.

> Yes:    The all-in price you see is the price you pay.
> No:     We're revolutionising live event ticketing forever.

### Warm, never twee
We sound like a person who actually goes to events, not like a brand consultant. We do not say "happenings". We do not say "vibe". We do not call our users "friends" or "fam".

> Yes:    Saturday is sold out. Friday still has tickets.
> No:     This Saturday's vibe is fully booked, fam!

### Specific, never generic
We name the cultures, the cities, and the dollar amounts. Generic copy is the failure mode of every competitor we surveyed. Specificity is our wedge.

> Yes:    Afrobeats in Melbourne, Bollywood in Toronto, Lunar New Year in San Francisco.
> No:     Discover the best events in your city for your taste.

### Plain, never decorative
Australian English (`organise`, `colour`, `realise`, `centre`). Sentence case headlines. No exclamation marks. No em-dashes. No en-dashes. Short sentences. Short verbs. If a marketing word and a normal word both fit, we use the normal word.

> Yes:    Squad booking. Your friends pay together.
> No:     Synergistic group purchasing. Your tribe transacts as one.

### Honest, never gimmicky
If we have a fee, we show it. If a tier is sold out, we say sold out. If a card says "selling fast" then it is, in fact, selling fast. We do not invent urgency. We do not invent scarcity. We do not bury fees on the next page.

> Yes:    Two tickets left at this price.
> No:     Hurry! Almost gone!

| all-in pricing                                   | Trust line, footer, pricing page, every event card           |
| no surprise fees                                 | Trust line, checkout, organiser pricing                      |

| guest checkout, guest order                      | The non-account path. Always honoured.                       |
| 2-tap payment                                    | Where the surface allows; otherwise "two-tap"                |
| keep more                                        | Organiser pitch (paired with "sell tickets")                 |
| ready when you are                               | Empty states, post-creation moments                          |
| we'll keep your seat                             | Reservation hold confirmations                               |
| you're in                                        | Order confirmation. Replaces "Thank you for your purchase!"  |

### Words and phrases we do not use

These appear nowhere in public copy. If a draft contains one, the draft is wrong.

- diaspora (in any tense or compound)
- em-dash, en-dash
- exclamation mark (except inside user-generated content the platform does not control)
- "your one-stop shop"
- "we revolutionise"
- "next-generation"
- "best-in-class"
- "cutting-edge"
- "seamless" (banned because every competitor uses it; it has lost meaning)
- "synergy", "synergistic"
- "world-class"
- "amazing", "awesome", "incredible"
- "curated", "hand-picked", "bespoke" (Eventbrite-coded)
- "fam", "tribe", "squad" (when used as a synonym for "user"; allowed as the literal feature name "Squad booking")
- "friends" as a generic addressee
- "click here", "learn more" (no-information links)
- "just" or "simply" (minimises difficulty for the reader)
- "vibe", "vibes" (Songkick-coded)
- "happenings", "things to do" (Eventbrite-coded)
- "next thing to do" (Eventbrite-coded)
- "tribe", "fan-first" as a tagline (DICE-coded; we say "fan-first" only inside CLAUDE.md and internal docs)
- "our community" used to flatter the reader
- emojis in marketing copy (allowed in user-generated content, banned in product chrome)

### Words to use carefully

| Word          | Use only when                                                                                            |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Discover      | Never as a primary CTA. Acceptable in editorial section heads sparingly.                                 |
| Explore       | Never as a primary CTA. Acceptable on rails labelled "Explore the cultures" or similar specific framing. |
| Featured      | Only when the surface algorithmically or editorially features events. Not as a synonym for "popular".    |
| Trending      | Only when backed by real signal (sales velocity, page views).                                            |
| Free          | The literal word, applied to literally free tickets. Not used as a hook for paid tiers.                  |
| Trusted       | Only with concrete proof on the same surface (organiser count, ticket count, payout volume).            |

## 5. CTA verbs

Primary CTAs use one of these verbs. Other verbs are not approved.

| Verb       | Where                                                                                  |
| ---------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Find       | Browse, search, hero secondary                                                          |
| Browse     | Catalog navigation, "Browse all events", "Browse by culture"                            |
| See        | Editorial CTAs ("See this week's events"), category drill-down                          |
| Get        | Tickets ("Get tickets"), apps ("Get the app")                                           |
| Buy        | Used only when explicitly transactional and free events are excluded from the surface   |
| Save       | Wishlist, favourite, "Save event"                                                       |
| Send       | Share flows. "Send to a friend". WhatsApp-first.                                        |
| Share      | Public share. "Share this event". WhatsApp, X, copy link.                               |
| Sell       | Organiser-side. "Sell tickets", "Start selling"                                         |
| Start      | "Start selling tickets", "Start your event". Always paired with a noun.                 |
| Sign in    | Auth pages only.                                                                        |
| Continue   | Multi-step flows where the next step is in the same task.                               |
| Confirm    | Final commit step before money moves or state changes.                                  |

Forbidden CTA verbs: Discover (as primary), Explore (as primary), Click, Tap, Submit (use Confirm or the specific verb), Engage, Connect (the verb), Unlock, Activate.

## 7. Punctuation rules

- **Period (.)**: end of sentences, including in headlines and CTAs that read like sentences ("Find your next event."). Not on section headings or navigation labels.
- **Comma (,)**: standard usage. Oxford comma optional but preferred when it removes ambiguity.
- **Colon (:)**: allowed in editorial copy. Avoid in CTAs.
- **Semicolon (;)**: avoid. Two short sentences read better.
- **Em-dash (—)**: forbidden. Anywhere. Public, internal, code comments, commit messages, docs. Hard rule.
- **En-dash (–)**: forbidden in copy. Allowed for date ranges in dynamic, machine-formatted strings only.
- **Hyphen (-)**: standard usage in compound modifiers ("all-in pricing", "fan-first").
- **Middle dot (·)**: separator for the cultures list and city rails.
- **Exclamation mark (!)**: forbidden in product chrome. Allowed in user-generated content the platform does not author.
- **Question mark (?)**: allowed in headers and search prompts. Used sparingly.
- **Quotation marks**: double for direct quotation, single for nested. Do not use "scare quotes" for emphasis. Use bold or italic if emphasis is required, but avoid both where possible.
- **Apostrophes**: standard. "you're", "we'll", "the organiser's account".
- **Ellipsis (...)**: avoid in marketing copy. Allowed in loading microcopy ("Loading your tickets...").

## 8. Cultural tone rules

- Never call any culture "exotic". Never describe a culture as "different" or "unique" by way of contrast to a default.
- Never use "ethnic" as an adjective. The cultures we serve are not "ethnic"; they are cultures.
- Never use "minority" or "underrepresented" in public copy. The Afrobeats fan is not a minority on this platform; they are the audience.
- Cultures are nouns. We say "Afrobeats events", not "Afrobeats-themed events". The events are the culture, not a costume.
- City names take their local form: Melbourne, Toronto, Sydney, Lagos, London, San Francisco, Auckland, Dubai. Never "the Big Apple", "the Harbour City", or local nicknames.
- Religious and cultural festival names are spelled correctly and consistently: Lunar New Year, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Diwali, Vesak, Hanukkah, Easter, Christmas, Holi. We never abbreviate religious holiday names.
- We do not editorially comment on the cultural meaning of a festival inside event cards or hero copy. We let the events speak.

## 10. Voice in code, internal docs, and commits

The voice rules above apply to every public surface and every customer-facing string. Internal voice (build standards, this document, code comments, progress logs, commit messages, dashboard developer screens) follows the same rules with two relaxations:

1. **"diaspora" is allowed in internal docs** that pre-date this sweep, where it is part of the historical record. New internal docs do not use it.
2. **Em-dashes remain forbidden everywhere**, internal or public. This is a hard rule and a memory entry.

Commit messages, like all copy, use Australian English, sentence case for the subject after the conventional-commits prefix, no em-dashes, no exclamation marks, no co-authorship attribution.

## 11. Why these rules exist

Every rule in this document came from a specific competitor failure or a specific EventLinqs strategic choice:

- The 4 to 6 word hero comes from the headline-length analysis in `research/findings.md` Q1.
- The CTA verb list comes from the Q2 analysis: "Discover" is soft because Eventbrite uses it as a hero verb; "Sign up" is friction because Songkick gates discovery behind it.
- The cultural tone rules exist because Eventbrite's "By Seasonal Moments" footer lists Halloween, St. Patrick's Day, Christmas, and Valentine's. That is the failure mode we will not repeat.
- The "no surprise fees" line answers Ticketmaster's litigated history of fees that move at checkout.
- The Humanitix-shape footer (Q4 analysis) is what we adopt because it reads at 320px and on a feature phone. Eventbrite's 10-column maximalist footer is what we reject.
- "You're in" replaces "Thank you for your purchase" because the moment after buying a ticket is a moment of joy, not commerce.
